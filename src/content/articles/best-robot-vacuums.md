## How we chose these robot vacuums

A robot vacuum lives or dies on two things most spec sheets bury: how reliably
it navigates your home, and how little maintenance it demands. Big suction
numbers make good marketing, but a robot that gets stuck under the same chair
every day, or that you have to empty after each run, is the one that ends up in
a cupboard.

So we weight our testing toward the things that decide whether you keep using
it. We run each robot across hard floors, low-pile and medium-pile carpet, and
around the cables, rug tassels and furniture legs of an ordinary home. We time
how long it takes to map a floor, how often it needs rescuing, and how much of
your week it hands back.

## What matters when you buy

### Navigation: LiDAR versus cameras

The single biggest driver of a good experience is navigation. **LiDAR** models
build an accurate map and clean in tidy, logical rows, in daylight or dark.
Cheaper **sensor- or camera-based** robots bounce around more and struggle in
low light. If your home has more than a couple of rooms, prioritise LiDAR.

### A self-emptying base is the real upgrade

Ask anyone who owns a robot vacuum what changed the game, and most say the
**self-emptying base**. Instead of emptying a small bin after every clean, the
robot empties itself into a larger bag you touch every few weeks. It is the
difference between a gadget and an appliance.

### Pet hair is about brushes, not just suction

If you have pets, look past the headline Pascal (Pa) figure. What actually keeps
a robot working is a **brush that resists tangling** — rubber or hybrid designs
shed hair rather than wrapping it into an immovable knot. High suction helps, but
a tangled brush stops cleaning entirely.

### Mopping: helpful, not transformative

Robot mopping has improved enormously, and the best models now lift or wash
their own pads. It is great for maintenance passes on sealed hard floors. But if
you have stubborn, stuck-on messes, a robot mop supplements a proper clean — it
does not replace one.

## Who should buy which

- **Most people:** the best overall pick hits the sweet spot of navigation,
  cleaning and low upkeep without paying for features you will rarely use.
- **Pet owners:** prioritise the anti-tangle pick — it is the difference between
  a robot that keeps going and one that jams by Tuesday.
- **Small homes and tight budgets:** a good budget robot cleans well. You simply
  trade some navigation smarts and the self-empty base for a much lower price.

Whatever you choose, set realistic expectations: a robot vacuum keeps your floors
consistently clean between deeper cleans. Pair it with a good upright or stick
vacuum for the occasional heavy job, and it earns its place quickly.
