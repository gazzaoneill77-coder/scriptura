## Start with how you'll actually use it

The robot vacuum market is deliberately confusing. Dozens of near-identical
models compete on ever-bigger suction numbers, while the things that decide
whether you'll love or abandon a robot barely get a mention. This guide cuts
through it with four questions, answered in order. Work through them and the
right robot for your home falls out naturally.

## Question 1: What are your floors like?

Everything starts here. Mostly **hard floors** are the easiest case — almost any
robot copes, and mopping becomes worth considering. Lots of **carpet**, especially
medium or deep pile, demands stronger suction and a brush that digs in. Mixed
floors are the norm, so look for a robot that senses carpet and boosts power
automatically.

## Question 2: Do you want it to empty itself?

This is the question that most changes day-to-day life. A **self-emptying base**
tips the robot's dust into a larger bag you empty every few weeks, instead of a
small onboard bin you'd otherwise empty after most cleans. It adds cost and takes
up floor space, but it's the feature owners rave about. If your budget stretches
to one thing, make it this.

## Question 3: Do you need mopping?

Modern hybrids vacuum and mop in one pass, and the best now wash or lift their
own pads to avoid dragging dirt onto carpet. If you have sealed hard floors and
want a light daily freshen-up, it's genuinely useful. If your home is mostly
carpet, or you expect a mop to tackle dried-on messes, skip it and put the money
toward better navigation.

## Question 4: What's your budget?

Now — and only now — set a price. With the first three answers in hand you know
which features you're paying for, so you won't overspend on mopping you won't use
or a base you don't want.

A useful rule of thumb: **spend on navigation and upkeep, not on suction.** A
robot with excellent mapping and a self-empty base at moderate suction will serve
you far better than a poorly-navigating robot boasting a huge Pascal figure.

## The features that are mostly noise

- **Enormous suction numbers.** Beyond a sensible threshold, brush design and
  navigation matter more.
- **Voice assistants built into the robot.** You'll control it from the app or
  your phone; on-robot voice is rarely used.
- **App "AI" gimmicks.** Reliable mapping and no-go zones are worth having;
  novelty features usually aren't.

Answer the four questions honestly and you'll spend on what you'll feel every
day — and ignore what you won't.
